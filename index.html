01NexusNet — Professional Networking Platform

Overview

NexusNet is JRXDev's flagship product — a professional networking platform built from first principles around human behavior, not engagement optimization. Where other platforms reward performance, NexusNet rewards genuine connection.

We started with a simple hypothesis: the reason people find professional networking exhausting is because existing tools have misaligned incentives. NexusNet is designed to invert those incentives entirely.

The Problem

Professional networking is broken.

Over 18 months of user research, we interviewed more than 200 professionals across industries. The finding was consistent: people don't hate networking — they hate how current platforms make them feel. NexusNet is our answer.

Our Approach

NexusNet removes follower counts, algorithmic feeds, and vanity metrics entirely. Introductions require mutual opt-in. Every interaction is intentional.

Why professional networking is broken — and how we plan to fix it

The modern professional network has become a performance stage. This is our manifesto.

JRXDev Team8 min read

There is a specific feeling that most professionals know well: the hollow sensation after spending twenty minutes crafting a post for a professional networking site, hitting publish, watching the likes roll in, and feeling nothing. Maybe worse than nothing.

We have talked to hundreds of people about this. The response is always the same. "I hate LinkedIn but I have to use it." "It makes me feel like I am performing my career rather than having one."

The incentive problem

The root cause is not the people using these platforms. It is the platforms themselves. Professional networking tools have been built around maximizing time on site, content creation, and social pressure. These incentives directly conflict with what professionals actually want.

What NexusNet is doing differently

NexusNet has no feed, no follower count, no algorithmic ranking. Instead it surfaces relevant connections based on what you are actually working on and who you know in common. Introductions require mutual opt-in.

This is our manifesto

We are building NexusNet for the professional who is tired of performing. Who wants to build genuine relationships and find real opportunities without the ambient pressure of every other platform in this space.

Design · March 2026

Designing for trust: the anti-dark-pattern playbook

The design principles we use to build software people trust.

JRXDev Design6 min read

Dark patterns are everywhere. Subscription cancellations hidden behind seven screens. Consent dialogs where "Accept All" is large and obvious while "Manage Preferences" is small and confusing. We have built a list of every dark pattern we have encountered and use it as a checklist of things to actively avoid.

Principle 1: Clarity is a feature

Every interface decision that requires the user to think harder than necessary is a small trust withdrawal. We treat clarity as a core product feature. Every label is tested for comprehension. Every destructive action requires confirmation.

Principle 2: The honest empty state

When we have not earned anything to show you yet, we say so directly and tell you exactly what to do next. No manufactured social proof, no fake urgency.

Principle 3: Make leaving easy

NexusNet has one-click account deletion that is prominent, immediate, and permanent. We believe this is the right thing to do — and it also makes people more comfortable staying.

Engineering · February 2026

Privacy-first architecture: building for data ownership

How we structure backend systems to give users genuine control over their data from day one.

JRXDev Engineering10 min read

Most companies treat privacy as a compliance exercise. We made a different choice: privacy is a first-class architectural constraint, not a feature added later.

Data minimization in practice

Our rule: if we cannot articulate a specific, near-term use case for a piece of data, we do not collect it. This means a smaller attack surface, simpler compliance obligations, and users who trust us with the data we do collect.

Zero-knowledge architecture

For genuinely sensitive data, we use zero-knowledge architecture. Data is encrypted client-side before reaching our servers. JRXDev cannot read it. This is the only honest way to promise users that their private data is private.

The deletion problem

Most "delete account" implementations retain data for months. We implemented real deletion: when you delete your account, a cascade of deletion jobs runs across every system. When it is done, it is genuinely done.

Research · February 2026

What 200 interviews taught us about professional connection

Findings from our foundational user research study reshaping NexusNet's core features.

JRXDev Research7 min read

Over six months, we spoke with more than 200 professionals across 14 industries. We came in with hypotheses. Most of them were wrong.

Finding 1: People don't hate networking. They hate the format.

Almost universally, participants valued professional relationships but expressed frustration with the platforms meant to facilitate those connections. The problem is not connection itself — it is the awkward, performative format platforms impose on it.

Finding 2: The best introductions come with context.

The most valuable professional introductions always included specific details about why these two people should meet. Cold introductions rarely led anywhere. This directly shaped NexusNet's introduction feature.

Finding 3: Visibility pressure is exhausting.

The pressure to maintain visibility on professional platforms was actively harming participants' relationships with their own work. We are building something that inverts this entirely.

Building a company culture that actually lives its values

The intentional steps we have taken to build a team that reflects our human-centered philosophy.

JRXDev Team5 min read

It is easy to write values on a wall. It is much harder to make decisions that reflect those values when they conflict with convenience, speed, or short-term gain.

Hiring for values alignment

Every person we have brought onto JRXDev has been evaluated not just on their technical abilities, but on whether they genuinely share our belief that software should serve people. We have passed on highly skilled candidates who seemed motivated primarily by building impressive technology rather than its impact.

Decision-making that centers impact

We have implemented a simple practice for every significant decision: before finalizing, we ask who does this help and is it actually helping them? Making it explicit has caught several decisions that would have been made automatically without that check.

We build a culture where mistakes are examined, not hidden. When something goes wrong, we do a genuine retrospective and publish findings internally. The goal is to learn.

Product · January 2026

The metrics we chose not to track — and why

The vanity metrics we deliberately excluded, and what we measure instead.

JRXDev Product6 min read

What you measure shapes what you build. A product team optimizing for daily active users will build very different features than one optimizing for reported satisfaction.

What we don't track: time on app

Time on app is the canonical vanity metric. It is easy to juice with more notifications and feed content. We do not track it because optimizing for it would directly conflict with what we are building. NexusNet should be useful quickly and then get out of your way.

What we don't track: follower counts

NexusNet does not have follower counts visible to users, and we do not track them internally. The number of people who followed a profile tells us almost nothing about whether that person is getting value from the platform.

What we do track: connection quality

We survey users about whether connections made through NexusNet have been valuable. We track introductions that lead to subsequent conversation. We track whether users report finding opportunities. These metrics are harder to game and directly correspond to the value we are trying to create.

Vision · January 2026

Where we see professional software in 2030

A forward-looking perspective on how professional tools will evolve and the role JRXDev intends to play.

JRXDev Team9 min read

Predicting the future of software is a fool's game, and we are going to do it anyway — with the caveat that we are more interested in the future we want to build than the one current trends suggest.

The backlash to attention economics is underway

The awareness of manipulative design patterns, the fatigue with notification-driven products, the growing preference for tools that respect your time — these trends are real and accelerating. By 2030, products built on attention extraction will face significant headwinds from users and regulation alike.

Professional tools will become more context-aware

The next generation of professional software will surface relevant connections and opportunities based on what you are actually trying to accomplish. NexusNet is building toward this: connection recommendations based on your current projects, not your past activity.

The future JRXDev is building toward

By 2030, we want JRXDev to be known as the company that proved you could build great, profitable software by taking users seriously. That being genuinely useful is a better business model than being addictive. We are early. We are small. But we believe the direction is right.
